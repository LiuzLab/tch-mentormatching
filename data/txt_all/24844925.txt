overview

Throughout my training and education, I have taken a special interest in the
psychosocial burdens of genetic disease and ways that providers can mitigate
these burdens. During medical school, I worked in outreach, executing a series
of writing workshops for children and adults affected by genetic diagnoses.
Since then, I have moved on to academic investigations of the psychosocial
burden of genetic diagnoses. My research has included analysis of the utility
of certain patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs) in osteogenesis
imperfecta; assessment of psychosocial functioning in the rare disease TANGO2;
and analysis of sequencing data and seeing patients for the Undiagnosed
Disease Network.  
My research continues to focus on quality of life in patients and families
affected by genetic conditions, and the ways in which clinicians and
researchers can measure and improve quality of life. My clinical practice
focuses on general pediatric genetics, and I have received specialized
training in skeletal dysplasias.  
Outside of medicine, I also write personal essays. I have had essays published
in several literary magazines, and my writing informs my desire to hear
patients' stories.
